Unboxing the Box Spread: An Advanced Options Strategy

For those who have spent their careers as option market makers or traders on prop desks, the concept of a box spread will be very familiar. However, if you're not within these circles, the box spread might seem like a new concept, even though it has been around for several decades.

So, what is a box spread? Simply put, it's a four-leg option trade that aims to secure a fixed payoff at a specific time in the future. It might sound complicated at first glance, but it's quite straightforward when broken down. The box spread is a synthetic loan that can be compared to a zero-coupon bond that is initially bought at a discount and steadily increases in price as it approaches its maturity date.

Yes, I know...this sounds boring. So why do I bring this up?

Two main reasons. Firstly, the current interest rate environment very interesting. Negative interest rates seem like an absurdity of the past. Global inflation and the debt ceiling issue are driving interest rate volatility higher. At the same time the interest rate differences along the curve offer great arbitrage opportunities that we can exploit trading box spreads.
Secondly, box spreads can be more tax efficient - at least they are in my tax jurisdiction (Luxembourg). While interest income (e.g. from a term deposit) is taxable in most tax jurisdictions, the profit of a long box spread is only realized at maturity. So, if you live in a country like Luxembourg where realized gains of an investment holding period above 6 months are tax exempt you better buy a box spread than lending the money to your bank.

The Mechanics of a Box Spread

There is a wide range of resources on the web where you will find an explanation of the box spread strategy (e.g. see Investopedia). In contrast to most articles explaining the box spread as a combination of a bull call spread and a bearish put spread, I find it more intuitive to think of the box as the combination of a synthetic long at a lower strike price (K1) and a synthetic short position at a higher strike (K2).

The synthetic long position consists of buying a call option (unlimited upside, but limited downside in the amount of the call premium paid) and selling a put option (limited upside in the amount of the premium received from selling the put, unlimited downside).
And vice versa the synthetic short consists of buying a put and selling a call.

The combination of the two synthetic positions is market neutral where the profit is equal to (K2 - K1) the difference between the two strike prices.

 

The S&P 500 Index closed at 4124 index points last Friday. Suppose we entered a synthetic long position at $4100 (K1) and a synthetic short position at $4200 (K2).

Example 1: S&P500 Index at 4000

When the index is at 4000 (below K1), the synthetic long position at K1 results in a loss because the index is below the strike price. The payoff would be 4000 - 4100 = -$100. The synthetic short position at K2, however, would be profitable, with a payoff of 4200 - 4000 = $200. Leading to the expected profit of K2 - K1 = 4100 - 4000 = $100.

Example 2: S&P500 Index at 4300

When the index is at 4300 (above K2), the synthetic long position at K1 results in a profit because the index is above the strike price. The payoff would be 4300 - 4100 = $200. However, the synthetic short position at K2 would result in a loss, with a payoff of 4200 - 4300 = -$100. Again leading to the expected profit of $100.

 

The Risks

 

1. Opportunity Costs

A lot of resources covering the box spread mention that its an arbitrage strategy and a long box is essentially risk-free. First of all, I have a problem with the word 'arbitrage' most articles use it in the context with box spreads. When you trade a box spread you are essentially lending money and you receive the difference of the two strike prices (K2 - K1) upon the expiration date. Lending money, however, occurs opportunity costs and one should not talk about a zero-cost or arbitrage strategy when simply buying a long box spread. Not our problem: In the course of this series we will compare the implied interest rates of various box spread options with opportunities we can find in the money market and seek for real arbitrage profits.

2. Counterparty Risk

While the market risk of a box spread is zero, it should be mentioned that you or every option trader has the risk that the counterparty fails to deliver their contractual obligations. The counterparty in question for box spreads is the Options Clearing Corporation (OCC) that is responsible for clearing option contracts. The risk of a failed settlement is, however, very minimal as the OCC requires investors to provide a margin and the CBOE is also providing additional margin that is used to clear the options traded. In fact, the OCC has the same credit rating as US Sovereign Debt and the Federal Reserve will likely intervene if anything goes wrong as the OCC is classified as a Systemically Important Financial Market Utility (SIFMU).

3. Implementation Risk

The box spread is an advanced option strategy and implementation errors can lead to high financial losses.
The best known example for a failed implementation is The legend of 1RONYMAN (the link for a funny video summarizing what went wrong). 1RONYMAN was a member of the Reddit community WallStreetBets who realized a loss of more than $57,000 on $5,000 equity, because he traded American style options that can be executed before expiration date. That is why you should only implement a box for European style options.
