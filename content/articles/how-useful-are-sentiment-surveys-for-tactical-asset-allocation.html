This year I was invited to contribute to the S&P Global Investment Manager Index (IMI) which is a survey-based indicator of market sentiment derived from active fund managers at institutional investment firms. IMI is designed to provide insights into what’s driving markets as well as forward-looking investment appetite and risk outlook for U.S. equities. The monthly survey asks respondents for their subjective view on a range of topics over the next 30 days including:

  • Market performance and key drivers
  • Upside and downside risks
  • Sector outlooks and preferences
  • Current risk appetite
  • Return expectations
  • Forces driving valuations
  • Topical questions based on current developments and market-moving news

In return for answering the survey S&P Global grants participants free access to the report which is usually $100. If you would like to contribute and get access you can sign up here: https://www.spglobal.com/marketintelligence/en/mi/Info/0920/imi-survey-request.htmlThe

This Month's Survey


This month was the first time that I investigated the report in detail and the bearish readings caught my attention. The 9-page report has a nice layout and gets you excited about how institutional asset managers view the prospects of the US equity market. For investors it can be helpful to know how the big money is positioned and even better if there was some indicator how this money will be positioned next months. Eyeballing over the report I wondered if such a sentiment survey can be  this leading indicator and help with our way to superior returns.

In the middle of the report you can find this graph depicting the investor preferences for each sector for the subsequent 30 days. At first glance it looks a bit cluttered, but after a closer look I found it really interesting.

L/S Factor Approach

In order to evaluate whether or not the survey results are added value for a tactical asset allocation we can approach the analysis in different ways. We could start with individual weights for each sector and then penalize these weights for a higher (worse) sector rank, thereby effectively going overweight the preferred sectors and underweight the least preferred sectors. Or simply conduct the analysis in a long/short manner extracting the alpha between the preferred and least preferred. Since we are not creating a trading strategy or portfolio around this, the long/short analysis is a good starting point.
We will go long sectors 1-5 and short sectors 7-11, leaving sector ranking 6 neutral. The difference between the two is the alpha.

# Define new DataFrame with index values and column names of DataFrame "imi"
ls = pd.DataFrame(index=imi.index, columns=imi.columns)
# set values to np.NaN
ls[:] = np.NaN
# iterate over the rows and calculate the portfolio performance of the L/S portfolio

for i in range(len(first_days)):
    start_date = first_days[i]
  
    if i == len(first_days) - 1:
        end_date = df.index[-1]
    else:
        end_date = first_days[i + 1]
    
    # Filter rows of 'df' based on the date range
    date_range_df = df[(df.index >= start_date) & (df.index < end_date)]

    # Calculate the average returns for the best and worst 5 sectors
    long_sectors = imi.iloc[i].nsmallest(5).index
    short_sectors = imi.iloc[i].nlargest(5).index

    for sector in long_sectors:
        ls.loc[ls.index[i], sector] = (date_range_df[sector].iloc[-1] / date_range_df[sector].iloc[0]) - 1

    for sector in short_sectors:
        ls.loc[ls.index[i], sector] = (date_range_df[sector].iloc[-1] / date_range_df[sector].iloc[0]) - 1

    long_returns = np.mean((date_range_df[long_sectors].iloc[-1] / date_range_df[long_sectors].iloc[0]) -1)
    short_returns = np.mean((date_range_df[short_sectors].iloc[-1] / date_range_df[short_sectors].iloc[0]) -1)

    ls.loc[ls.index[i],"LS_Return"] = long_returns - short_returns

Conclusion

In the period of analysis the sector ranking by investor preferences of the S&P Global Investment Manager Index would have been helpful for a tactical asset allocation. The cumulative alpha for the year 2022 was above 13% and investors using the IMI results had the chance to strongly outperform the S&P 500 index. However, the analysis discovers that during short term market regime shifts the strategy to over/underweight an investmentportfolio based on sentiment can lead to a large drawdown and investors are not advised to forego the diversification potential as is the case in the long/short approach.
