Even after a whopping increase of 4,5% to a FED funds rate of 4,75% within 12 months, inflation is still hot. A popular bet at the start of 2023 was whether inflation or the key interest rate will cross the 5% level earlier. The last inflation data came in at 6% YoY headline CPI for February and the next rate decision will be this Wednesday 22nd of March. Two weeks ago, market participants were pretty sure the key interest rate will win this race as a 50bps hike was seen with a probability of 80%. Now, with the collapse of the Silicon Valley Bank the trust in other regional banks in the US is severely damaged and the FED had to step in to provide liquidity.

This marks a temporary end to the bank's quantitative tightening policy. More than half of the $600 billion balance sheet reduction has already evaporated. Broken trust in the banking system is a serious problem and the central bank has to act, yet, a prolonged quantitative easing increases the risk that inflationary pressures will not be brought under control.

What does this mean for investors?

Fighting and controlling inflation is a binary game. Either the central bank wins or loses. If the Fed stops tightening too early it risks losing this time and - although the financial markets and the banking system gain time - the battle to fight inflation will be even harder if it is postponed. Investors could find themselves in a similar situation as in the 1970s.

 

The 1970s were a time of economic turmoil, marked by rising inflation, multiple recessions, and poor stock performance. This period followed a prosperous decade in the 1960s, which saw steady economic growth and a bullish stock market. During the 1970s, however, a series of events—including the oil crisis, the end of the Bretton Woods system, and high government spending—led to rampant inflation and a loss of confidence in the financial system.

Investors during this challenging decade had to navigate an unpredictable landscape, as inflation and economic instability took a toll on stock market returns. The resilience and patience exhibited by long-term investors during this era, however, offer valuable lessons for today's investors as they face similar challenges.

The Road Ahead: Learning from the Past and Preparing for the Future

Our Monte Carlo simulation analysis of the 1970s provides a stress test for today's expected returns and highlights the potential consequences of losing the battle against inflation. As the Fed grapples with the delicate balance between providing liquidity to the banking system and controlling inflation, investors must be prepared for the possibility of a repeat of the 1970s scenario.

The Probability of Success/ Profit


In our Monte Carlo Simulation we are randomly picking days to enter the stock market (S&P 500 Index) and calculate the probability that the investment is profitable as well as the average return for six holding periods.

The results demonstrate a clear pattern: as the holding period increased, both the probability of success and the average returns improved. This trend emphasizes the importance of patience and resilience in the world of investing, even during challenging market conditions.

The Takeaway: Resilience in the Face of Adversity

The analysis of the 1970s offers valuable insights for investors navigating today's market landscape. By staying invested for the long term, investors can benefit from the potential growth of the market, even during tough times. Our stress test shows that low investment horizons have a high probability of losing money and the expected returns of the intermediary holding periods are somewhat dissapointing. The real returns (adjusted for inflation) would even be negative. Nevertheless, this exercise demonstrates that patience and long-term investment strategies can lead to improved average returns and a higher success rate.
