Diversification might be the only free lunch in finance. So I don’t think it is enough to diversify with a basket of stocks across sectors and sizes (small- to large-cap). I don’t even think diversifying by asset class is sufficient.

We saw why in the recent sell-off. The moment the market started pricing rising interest rates, everything went out the door at once — bonds, gold, crypto and stocks. When the discount rate moves, cross-asset correlations snap toward one and the “diversified” portfolio behaves like a single, leveraged bet on liquidity.

So I believe you have to diversify in the widest sense possible:

  • by instrument — cash securities (stocks, bonds), options, futures;
  • by style — momentum, value, carry, quality;
  • by strategy — buy & hold, mean reversion, trend following;
  • by timeframe — intraday to multi-year.

Sounds complicated? It is. But that’s what this site is about.

Where to start?

You have to start somewhere. And as we know, time in the market beats timing the market — a point I made when I showed that even trend signals work best with heavy smoothing (see Market Timing — Consider Additional Smoothing When Using Trend Indicators). We want to be invested in the market.

The base strategy is therefore a long equity position. Every other sleeve — the trend models, the short-vol books, the mean-reversion baskets — gets layered on top of this foundation. And because everything else adds complexity, the foundation itself should be as simple as it possibly can be.

The base strategy should be as simple as it gets. That means one broad, liquid, low-cost equity ETF.

Which one? The obvious candidates are the two most-traded equity ETFs on the planet: SPY (S&P 500) and QQQ (Nasdaq-100). Let’s actually look at the data instead of arguing about it.

SPY vs QQQ: more return, more pain

A few lines of Python pull the full common history (QQQ launched in March 1999), compute the total-return path on a log scale, and a rolling 1-year annualised volatility underneath it.

import yfinance as yf, numpy as np

px = yf.download(["SPY", "QQQ"], start="1999-03-10",
                 auto_adjust=True)["Close"].dropna()
rets = px.pct_change().dropna()

ann_ret = lambda r: (1 + r).prod() ** (252 / len(r)) - 1
ann_vol = lambda r: r.std() * np.sqrt(252)

# rolling 1-year volatility for the lower panel
roll_vol = rets.rolling(252).std() * np.sqrt(252)

The picture is unambiguous. Over ~27 years QQQ turned $100 into roughly $1,640 versus about $920 for SPY. But look at the lower panel: QQQ’s rolling volatility sits above SPY’s in almost every regime, and its dot-com drawdown reached an 83% peak-to-trough loss against SPY’s “merely” 55%.

This is the whole point. QQQ is the higher-octane engine, but it’s also ~1.4× as volatile. Putting 100% of your capital into either ETF at face value is a category error: you’re choosing a return and a risk level in a single click, when those two decisions should be made separately. There is no reason to deploy 100% of capital in SPY just because it “feels” safer, and no reason to white-knuckle 100% QQQ just because it returns more.

Separate the two decisions: volatility matching

The fix is to size the position by risk, not by dollars. We pick a target volatility — here, simply SPY’s own volatility — and scale QQQ exposure down until it carries the same risk, parking the rest in cash:

# weight that makes QQQ carry SPY's volatility
w = ann_vol(rets["SPY"]) / ann_vol(rets["QQQ"])   # -> ~0.72

# vol-matched sleeve: w in QQQ, (1-w) in cash (0% assumed)
qqq_vm = rets["QQQ"] * w
print(w, ann_vol(qqq_vm), ann_vol(rets["SPY"]))   # vols now equal

The result. Matching QQQ to SPY’s ~19.3% volatility takes only about 72% of capital in QQQ, with the remaining ~28% sitting in cash. That vol-matched sleeve delivers essentially the same long-run return as a fully-invested SPY (8.4% vs 8.5% annualised) at the same volatility — while freeing up roughly a quarter of the portfolio.

That freed capital is exactly the raw material the rest of this site is built on. It can sit in cash earning the risk-free rate (we conservatively assumed 0% above), or — far more interestingly — fund the diversifying sleeves: trend, carry, short-vol, mean reversion. Same equity risk, same equity return, plus room for the only free lunch in finance.

A word of honesty about the regime

Notice that vol-matching over the full sample gives ~72% capital and merely matches SPY’s return — it does not beat it. That’s because the 1999 start drags the brutal dot-com collapse into the numbers, the one episode where the Nasdaq’s extra risk was not rewarded. It’s a feature, not a bug: it’s precisely why we vol-manage the sleeve and diversify beyond it.

If we re-run the same volatility match over calmer, more recent windows, the case for the vol-matched Nasdaq gets stronger, not weaker — it produces higher returns than SPY at equal risk:

I won’t cherry-pick a window; the conservative full-sample read — equal return, equal risk, ~28% of capital freed — already makes the decision for us.

The verdict

My base equity sleeve is a volatility-managed long in the Nasdaq-100 (QQQ), sized to the risk I actually want to run rather than to the cash I happen to have. It is the simplest possible foundation, it is at least as efficient as the default S&P 500 buy-and-hold, and — crucially — it leaves capital on the table for everything else we’re going to build.

You have to start somewhere. We start here.
